Why High Performers Clash: The Narcissism of Small Differences

July 8, 2025 3 min read Selfmusing by Jiv

Top-performing teams don’t collapse because of incompetence. They collapse because high performers, often cut from the same cloth, begin to fixate on superficial differences like who uses what framework, whose email tone sounds more ‘client-ready’ or who presents first. These “divides” are magnified to defend a fragile sense of individuality in a group where everyone is similarly accomplished. The smaller the difference, the louder the conflict is, because it becomes a proxy for identity, status and control.

Coined by Freud, this concept of the narcissism of small differences describes how individuals become most competitive with those most like themselves. In elite environments like consulting, finance and tech, where team members share similar academic backgrounds, ambition and values, this dynamic quietly erodes collaboration. The more alike people are, the more they grasp for distinctiveness, to stand out for identity preservation.

However, for leadership, the cost is substantial. These subtle ego-driven clashes drain morale and stall progress on otherwise high-functioning teams. What’s worse is that these conflicts often fly under the radar by being polite on the surface but toxic underneath.

What can you do about it?

Promoting healthy competition is essential because it can drive efficiency, sharpen problem-solving and also push individuals to excel. But when competition becomes the default culture, it risks pitting colleagues against each other rather than aligning them toward a shared goal. Leaders must create systems that differentiate roles, clarify decision-making authority and reward collaborative wins .

Individuals, too, must take ownership. Recognizing when ego and not logic is fueling disagreement is critical. The ability to zoom out and re-anchor in the team’s purpose rather than personal territory is a mark of maturity and leadership readiness.

This isn’t just about team harmony, it’s more about performance.  In consulting, where reputations hinge on collaboration under pressure, the ability to recognize and diffuse subtle ego dynamics is as critical as any analytical framework. Teams fail from unmanaged friction. Understanding and managing the narcissism of small differences isn’t a soft skill but now it’s an operational necessity. And in a world where talent is expensive and culture is fragile, that awareness is more than just good leadership, it’s a strategic edge.
