Who Keeps Sora Safe?

December 14, 2025 5 min read Selfmusing by Jiv

When OpenAI launched Sora, its text‑to‑video model, the internet responded exactly as expected: awe, excitement, and a rush to experiment. Within days, users were generating shockingly realistic clips — some playful, some unsettling, many involving copyrighted characters and brand mascots that were never meant to be there in the first place.

OpenAI’s response was telling. Sora was framed not as a risky experiment, but as “a new kind of interactive fan fiction”. A creative playground. A collaborative ecosystem. A space where brands would soon have “granular control” over how their characters appeared.

That framing is what made me pause.

This piece grew out of a critical essay I wrote for IST 343: Data in Society, taught by Prof. Jasmina Tacheva at Syracuse University. The course pushed us to look beyond what data technologies promise and ask what they hide. Sora, to me, became a perfect case study.

The Illusion of Control

Language matters. When platforms use words like participation, co‑creation, and control, they borrow from traditions that were originally meant to redistribute power. In Sora’s case, that language suggests brands and users are co‑authors of the system—empowered collaborators shaping how AI behaves.

In practice, Sora has already been flooded with unauthorized uses of copyrighted characters. Even OpenAI admits that harmful or inappropriate content will “inevitably slip through.” The gap between what is promised and what actually exists is not accidental, it’s a classic example of AI hype: inflating technical readiness while downplaying social, legal, and ethical limits.

The interface feels friendly. The rhetoric feels reassuring. The infrastructure underneath is far messier.

Fauxtomation and Invisible Labor

What makes Sora work isn’t magic—and it isn’t full automation either.

Behind every “safe” output is a hidden layer of human labor: content moderators, data annotators, legal reviewers, and policy workers who clean, flag, correct, and contain what the system produces. This is often called ghost work—essential labor that remains intentionally invisible so the technology can appear autonomous.

This performance of automation has a name: fauxtomation. It’s the illusion that machines are doing the work alone, when in reality humans are constantly intervening to prevent harm. When that labor disappears from the story, accountability disappears with it. Responsibility quietly shifts away from the platform and onto users, brands, and unseen workers who absorb the emotional, legal, and ethical burden of keeping the system “safe.”

Who Bears the Risk?

Sora’s narrative makes participation look playful but the risks are unevenly distributed.

- Brands face reputational damage when mascots appear in violent, sexual, or political contexts.
- Artists and creators see their styles absorbed and remixed without consent or attribution.
- Users are nudged into a false sense of permission, unsure where creativity ends and infringement begins.
- Workers shoulder the hidden labor of moderation and enforcement, largely out of sight.

When platforms obscure these dynamics, they encourage automation bias—our tendency to trust machine outputs as neutral or objective, even when real people are quietly making judgment calls behind the scenes.

As part of this project, I proposed a simple intervention: What if we asked, publicly and repeatedly, “Who keeps Sora safe?”

The idea was a visual awareness campaign that places OpenAI’s promotional language—granular control, interactive fan fiction, brand collaboration—next to the human workflows that actually sustain those promises. Moderation queues. Escalation protocols. Legal review processes. Making labor visible doesn’t kill creativity. It builds data literacy. It helps us engage with AI more honestly, without mistaking infrastructure for magic.

Why This Analysis Matters Beyond the Classroom

One reason this project mattered to me is that the questions it raises are not abstract, they mirror the kinds of problems consultants and applied researchers confront every day.

At its core, this analysis treats Sora not as a product demo, but as a system: a combination of incentives, narratives, labor structures, risk allocation, and governance failures. That shift—from asking “Is this impressive?” to “How does this actually operate, who bears the costs, and where does accountability sit?”—is the same analytical move used in consulting, policy analysis, and strategy work.

Rather than accepting corporate messaging at face value, this project required:

- Deconstructing hype narratives and separating aspiration from operational reality
- Identifying hidden stakeholders and mapping uneven risk distribution
- Tracing incentives and externalities, especially where responsibility is quietly offloaded
- Designing an intervention, not just a critique, that could realistically shift public understanding

These are transferable skills. Whether evaluating an AI deployment, a platform governance model, or a brand partnership strategy, the ability to interrogate systems critically—and communicate those findings clearly to non-technical audiences—is essential.

This is also why the course mattered. IST 343: Data in Society, taught by Prof. Jasmina Tacheva, emphasized that technical sophistication alone is not enough. What distinguishes strong analysts, graduate students, and consultants is the ability to connect data systems to social consequences, institutional incentives, and ethical risk.

Sora isn’t unique. It represents a broader pattern in how AI is marketed today: polished narratives of empowerment layered over complex systems that rely on invisible human effort.

Learning to see those layers, to question who benefits, who bears risk, and who remains unseen is exactly what Data in Society challenged us to do. It’s also the mindset required in environments where decisions have real reputational, legal, and human consequences.

When technology feels effortless, who is making it so, and why aren’t we talking about them?
