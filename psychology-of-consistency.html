Psychology of Consistency: Why We Struggle to Stick to What We Start

July 3, 2025 4 min read Selfmusing by Jiv

How many times have you made a New Year’s resolution to finally hit the gym, only to find your membership card buried in your wallet come June, collecting dust like a souvenir from a trip you never took?

We all start with good intentions. We buy the planner, open the tab, tell a friend. But somewhere between starting and sustaining, something slips. Here are a few ideas I’ve been exploring.

The Intention-Action Gap

We plan, mean to follow through and yet we don’t. This gap between intention and action shows up everywhere. Sometimes it’s because life gets hectic. Other times it’s because we underestimate how hard sustained effort really is.

Our brains often mistake planning for doing. Just setting the goal can give us a small burst of satisfaction, which tricks us into feeling like we’ve already made progress. The result? A full planner and an untouched to-do list.

Delayed Gratification and the Marshmallow Test

You’ve probably heard of the Marshmallow Test. It measured whether kids could resist one marshmallow now for the promise of two later. But in 2018, researchers replicated it with a bigger and more diverse sample and the story changed.

The ability to delay gratification isn’t just willpower. It’s tied to trust, environment and whether the reward even feels real. Our brains naturally lean toward what’s available *now*. That’s why it’s so much easier to scroll than study or snack instead of stretch. Long-term goals don’t always offer instant wins, so we don’t always stay for the final payoff.

Fear of Imperfection

Sometimes we quit not because we’re lazy but because we care too much. We want the first draft to feel final, the project to be polished or the idea to land just right. Perfectionism creates pressure, which turns creative work into performance. Instead of trying and tweaking, we stall. It’s easier to wait than risk something flawed. And the longer we wait, the harder it feels to begin again.

It’s easy to think this is just a motivation problem and it shows up in more ways than we realize

The online course we never finish:  You start with energy, even take notes during the first few modules. Then in week three, assignments pile up and the course becomes another browser tab you avoid.

The half-filled journal:  That new notebook that felt full of potential, the same one in which you wrote for three days, then missed one, then another. Soon, it feels like you broke the streak but in reality your expectations just outdid your capacity.

The message left unsent:  You meant to call, to check in with someone you care about. But then things got busy. The longer it goes, the more awkward it feels. It’s not that the connection didn’t matter, it just slipped between the cracks of everything else.

What Actually Does Help?

Start smaller than you think you need to: Start by aiming to write a sentence, not a whole page. Start by doing a 5-minute stretch, not a 30-minute workout. Most of the resistance is in starting. When you can make the first step easy, the rest will follow. Celebrate the showing up, not just the result: We don’t always have control over the outcome, but we can recognize the effort. You don’t need a finished product to feel proud. Notice what’s getting in your way: Any triggers like boredom, burnout, fear of failure, even fear of success can shape our behavior. If you keep avoiding something, ask why. Not to shame yourself, but to understand the story underneath the silence.

For me, writing publicly is an experiment in consistency. It’s not always easy, but it’s a way to build trust with myself. To show up even when the words don’t flow perfectly.

Maybe the key to consistency isn’t working harder. Maybe it’s about noticing the gap, softening the pressure, being patient with yourself and returning anyway.

What’s something you started but struggled to continue? I’d love to hear your thoughts.
